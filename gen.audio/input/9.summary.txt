On December 3–4, 2025, the Indian Rupee fell sharply. It breached the ₹90 level for the first time ever. The intraday low reached ₹90.43 per US dollar. This marked a major economic milestone. The currency closed near ₹90.19 to USD on Wednesday and Thursday of 2025. This depreciation reflected long-term weakening of rupee against global currencies. Over 2025, the rupee lost about 5% in value. It became one of Asia's weakest performing currencies that year. In November and December 2025, foreign portfolio investors pulled out capital from India. They were concerned about global risk and rising interest rates in the United States. Higher yields on US bonds made dollar-denominated assets more attractive. This caused a sharp fall in investor confidence in Indian markets. Capital flight weakened the rupee by reducing demand for domestic currency. Trade negotiations between India and the United States remained stalled in late 2025. The U.S. imposed new tariffs on Indian goods, such as textiles, electronics, and machinery. This reduced competitiveness of Indian exporters. Investors interpreted these developments as negative for India’s trade balance. Exporters delayed converting revenues into rupees to benefit from a weaker currency later. Importers rushed to convert into dollars early. This created volatility in foreign exchange markets. The resulting imbalance strengthened dollar demand and pressured the Rupee downward. The Reserve Bank of India chose a cautious policy response to the rupee depreciation. It did not conduct large-scale foreign exchange operations to stabilize the currency. The RBI opted for selective measures instead of firm defense of levels. Its actions included managing volatility in forward markets and allowing some degree of natural adjustment. This restraint contrasted with historical precedents when RBI stepped decisively into currency market to prevent sharp falls. The fall in rupee value led to higher prices of imported goods. Fuel, medicines, electronics, raw materials, chemicals became costlier. This inflationary pressure affected household budgets. Students paying for foreign education faced higher tuition costs due to lower dollar conversion rates. Businesses with import-dependent supply chains faced rising production costs. Some export-oriented firms benefited from more rupees per dollar earned in overseas sales.
